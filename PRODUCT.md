# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Founders and CEOs of early-stage companies who are evaluating Mike for fractional CTO, technical co-founder, or contract engineering engagements. They arrive primarily through referrals and warm intros from Mike's network, not cold inbound. By the time they land here, they already know Mike exists — the site is their first read on his taste, judgment, and seniority.

Secondary context: peer engineers, former colleagues, and people Mike has worked with before who land here out of curiosity rather than intent to hire.

The job to be done is impression formation. A visitor finishes scrolling with a clear, accurate, slightly-better-than-expected mental model of who Mike is as a craftsman.

## Product Purpose

This is Mike's corner of the internet. It exists to be a true reflection of his taste and perspective as a senior software professional with 20+ years in the work — not a lead funnel, not a resume page, not a content machine.

Success is qualitative: a visitor feels something specific and accurate. Failure is generic: a visitor closes the tab having seen "another developer portfolio."

There is no conversion goal. The contact paths (email, Cal.com, LinkedIn, GitHub) exist for the people who want them; they are not optimized targets. The site can be a slower, weirder, more deliberate object than a typical portfolio because it isn't trying to maximize anything.

## Brand Personality

Playful, expressive, idiosyncratic.

Voice is direct, curious, and warm — the about-page line "I started my life in the shadow of my father, a career software developer and ravenous learner" is the register. Not formal, not jokey, not breathless. A person who has seen a lot and finds it interesting.

Reference lane: bold, brutalist, unexpected. Closer to a designer's personal site (Devine Lu Linvega, Tom Sachs portfolio style, anti-template structural choices) than to a SaaS landing. Choices a marketing team would never sign off on are the right kind of choices here. The site should look hand-built by one person with strong opinions, because it is.

Emotional goal: a visitor recognizes craft and conviction. They may not love every choice, but no choice feels defaulted into.

## Anti-references

- **The Tailwind Spotlight template.** This is what the site currently looks like. It's competent and the most-shipped personal-site template on the internet. Every choice that survives a redesign must justify why it isn't just Spotlight.
- **Generic SaaS landing pages.** Gradient hero text, three-column feature cards with icons, "Trusted by" logo strips, testimonial carousels, founder photo grids, CTA-stacked above-fold.
- **The hero-metric template.** "20+ years experience / 50+ projects / 12+ industries" big-number layouts. Mike's seniority shouldn't be a stat block.
- **Resume / CV pages.** Reverse-chronological work history, skill bars, "Download PDF" buttons. This site is not a job application.
- **Influencer / personal-brand-y energy.** Newsletter pop-ups, "join 10k founders" social proof, course funnels, audience-capture language.
- **AI-default aesthetics.** If a category-reflex check ("personal portfolio that's not Spotlight → brutalist-typographic") would predict the design, the design has stopped at the first reflex. Push past it.

## Design Principles

1. **The work is the pitch.** No "Trusted by" logos, no testimonial carousels, no funnels. Seniority shows up in the design choices, the writing, and the projects shown — not in social proof scaffolding.

2. **Break the template.** The Spotlight starting point is the negative space. Any design choice that would also work on a YC fintech landing or another developer's Spotlight clone should be rejected. Idiosyncrasy is the goal, not a side effect.

3. **A real person, not a brand.** Hand-built energy beats polished-template energy. The Calgary mountain photographs, the personal voice in the about page, the side projects — these are the load-bearing details. Lean into them.

4. **Reward the curious.** Visitors who scroll, hover, or come back should find things that visitors who skim do not. Unexpected transitions, small moments of personality, micro-interactions that demonstrate care. Easter eggs are on-brand here.

5. **Quality over volume.** Fewer pages, fewer cards, fewer items — each one earning its place. Generous whitespace where it earns the room; dense moments where they earn the room. Never pad to fill a layout.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA is the baseline floor, not a stretch goal.

- All interactive elements reachable and operable by keyboard. Focus states are visible and on-brand (not the browser default, but never invisible).
- Color contrast meets AA for body text, interactive controls, and meaningful icons. Decorative type can break this only if it is genuinely decorative and not load-bearing.
- `prefers-reduced-motion` is respected. Bold motion is on-brand, but a visitor who has opted out gets a calmer pass that conveys the same information.
- Semantic HTML throughout. Headings nest correctly, links are links, buttons are buttons. Decorative imagery has empty `alt`; meaningful imagery has descriptive `alt`.
- No reliance on color alone to convey state or meaning.
